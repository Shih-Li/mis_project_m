\subsubsection{Recovery across Design Configurations}
\label{app:detection-recovery}

The complete recovery grid reinforces the mechanism-dependent selectivity reported in Section~\ref{subsec:detection-performance}. For bad-leverage contamination, MIS recovered between (85\%) and (98\%) of the injected observations across the sample-size and contamination grid and generally exceeded Cook's distance, leverage, and DFBETAS (Figure~\ref{fig:02A-all-method-overlap}). The corresponding advantage over the strongest classical diagnostic was positive in every bad-leverage configuration, ranging from (8.0) to (14.9) percentage points (Table~\ref{tab:02A-mis-advantage-grid}). By contrast, Cook's distance and DFBETAS more frequently recovered vertical outliers, while leverage naturally recovered good-leverage observations. These differences reflect the broader notions of residual and leverage extremeness used by the classical diagnostics, whereas MIS prioritises observations that alter the coefficient of interest.

	\caption{Top-(k) recovery for MIS, Cook's distance, leverage, and DFBETAS across sample sizes, contamination proportions, and contamination mechanisms.}
	\label{fig:02A-all-method-overlap}
\end{figure}

\input{../../output/02_distributional/tables/supplement/02_tabA1_mis_advantage_grid.tex}

The distribution-specific results show that the bad-leverage advantage was not driven by a single error distribution. MIS recovery under bad leverage exceeded (97\%) for the normal, beta-logistic, mixed-normal, skewed-(t), and Pareto errors, while remaining above the best classical diagnostic in every considered distribution (Figure~\ref{fig:02A-distributional-robustness} and Table~\ref{tab:02A-detection-by-error}). The advantage was largest under GOLM and contaminated errors, at (21.1) and (17.3) percentage points, respectively. Performance weakened under GPD errors, where MIS recovered (78.5\%) of the injected observations and exceeded the best classical diagnostic by only (3.2) percentage points. Thus, the coefficient-targeted benefit of MIS remained broadly robust, although extremely heavy-tailed GPD errors reduced the separation between the competing procedures.

\subsubsection{Good-Leverage Mechanism Diagnostics}
\label{app:good-leverage-diagnostics}

The good-leverage diagnostics clarify why low overlap with the injected set should not be interpreted as a failure of MIS. Except under GPD errors, the MIS-selected observations had substantially lower leverage than the injected good-leverage observations but much larger residual magnitudes and absolute DFBETA values (Figure~\ref{fig:02A-good-leverage-mechanism} and Table~\ref{tab:02-good-leverage-mechanism}). For example, under normal errors the median leverage ratio was (0.07), whereas the median absolute DFBETA and residual ratios were (19.19) and (131.91), respectively. MIS therefore replaced observations that were extreme in predictor space but approximately aligned with the fitted relation with observations that exerted greater influence on the target coefficient.

	\label{fig:02A-good-leverage-mechanism}
\end{figure}

\input{../../output/02_distributional/tables/main/02_tab3_good_leverage_mechanism.tex}

The iteration-level leverage--residual relationship provides the same interpretation across the complete distributional designs (Figure~\ref{fig:02A-leverage-residual-scatter}). Most observations lie to the left of the leverage-equality line and above the residual-equality line, showing that MIS generally selected sets with lower leverage but substantially larger residual magnitudes than the injected good-leverage sets. Recovery increased when the injected observations themselves became more similar to the coefficient-influential sets identified by MIS, most visibly under GPD errors. This pattern confirms that MIS responds to joint coefficient influence rather than leverage alone.

\subsubsection{EVT Calibration, Convergence, and Computational Diagnostics}
\label{app:detection-evt-diagnostics}

The EVT component remained well calibrated under no contamination. Its empirical rejection rate was (4.5\%), close to the nominal (5\%) level (Table~\ref{tab:02-empirical-size-summary}). Under the same set-level rejection definition, the corresponding classical threshold events occurred considerably more frequently. This distinction supports the use of the EVT reference distribution when deciding whether the coefficient influence of an entire candidate set is unusually large.

\input{../../output/02_distributional/tables/main/02_tab2_empirical_size_summary.tex}

Convergence was uniformly high across the design grid and therefore does not explain the differences in detection performance. Rates remained between (99.1\%) and (100.0\%) across sample sizes, contamination proportions, and contamination mechanisms (Figure~\ref{fig:02A-evt-convergence} and Table~\ref{tab:02A-evt-convergence-grid}). The modest reduction at the largest sample size and contamination proportion was common across contaminated and uncontaminated designs and remained quantitatively small.

	\caption{EVT convergence rates across sample sizes, contamination proportions, and contamination mechanisms.}
	\label{fig:02A-evt-convergence}
\end{figure}

\input{../../output/02_distributional/tables/supplement/02_tabA2_evt_convergence_grid.tex}

The distribution-level EVT summaries further distinguish coefficient-relevant from coefficient-benign contamination (Table~\ref{tab:02A-gev-summary}). Under no contamination, rejection rates ranged from (4.0\%) to (5.3\%) across the error distributions. Under bad leverage, they exceeded (94\%) for every distribution except GPD, for which the rejection rate was (58.0\%). In contrast, good-leverage rejection remained between (1.0\%) and (3.4\%), consistent with its alignment with the fitted regression relation. The much larger GPD scale and location estimates reflect the scale of that heavy-tailed design and should not be compared directly with the corresponding parameters from the other error distributions.

\input{../../output/02_distributional/tables/supplement/02_tabA4_gev_summary.tex}

Finally, adaptive block selection preserved computational feasibility as the influential-set size increased (Table~\ref{tab:02A-block-count-summary}). The full (50) blocks were retained at contamination proportions of (0.5\%) and (1\%), while the median count declined to approximately (39)--(40) at (2.5\%), (19) at (5\%), and (9) at (10\%). Although the highest-contamination configurations therefore used fewer than ten blocks, their convergence rates remained above (99\%), indicating that the reduced block count did not produce substantial numerical instability.
