\subsection{Supplementary Detection Results}
\label{app:detection-results}

\subsubsection{Recovery across Design Configurations}
\label{app:detection-recovery}

The complete recovery grid reinforces the mechanism-dependent selectivity reported in Section~\ref{subsec:detection-performance}. For bad-leverage contamination, MIS recovered between (85\%) and (98\%) of the injected observations across the sample-size and contamination grid and generally exceeded Cook's distance, leverage, and DFBETAS (Figure~\ref{fig:02A-all-method-overlap}). The corresponding advantage over the strongest classical diagnostic was positive in every bad-leverage configuration, ranging from (8.0) to (14.9) percentage points (Table~\ref{tab:02A-mis-advantage-grid}). By contrast, Cook's distance and DFBETAS more frequently recovered vertical outliers, while leverage naturally recovered good-leverage observations. These differences reflect the broader notions of residual and leverage extremeness used by the classical diagnostics, whereas MIS prioritises observations that alter the coefficient of interest.

\begin{figure}[H]
	\centering
	\includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{../../output/02_distributional/figures/supplement/02_figA1_all_method_overlap_heatmaps.pdf}
	\caption{Top-(k) recovery for MIS, Cook's distance, leverage, and DFBETAS across sample sizes, contamination proportions, and contamination mechanisms.}
	\label{fig:02A-all-method-overlap}
\end{figure}

\input{../../output/02_distributional/tables/supplement/02_tabA1_mis_advantage_grid.tex}

The distribution-specific results show that the bad-leverage advantage was not driven by a single error distribution. MIS recovery under bad leverage exceeded (97\%) for the normal, beta-logistic, mixed-normal, skewed-(t), and Pareto errors, while remaining above the best classical diagnostic in every considered distribution (Figure~\ref{fig:02A-distributional-robustness} and Table~\ref{tab:02A-detection-by-error}). The advantage was largest under GOLM and contaminated errors, at (21.1) and (17.3) percentage points, respectively. Performance weakened under GPD errors, where MIS recovered (78.5\%) of the injected observations and exceeded the best classical diagnostic by only (3.2) percentage points. Thus, the coefficient-targeted benefit of MIS remained broadly robust, although extremely heavy-tailed GPD errors reduced the separation between the competing procedures.

\subsubsection{Good-Leverage Mechanism Diagnostics}
\label{app:good-leverage-diagnostics}

The good-leverage diagnostics clarify why low overlap with the injected set should not be interpreted as a failure of MIS. Except under GPD errors, the MIS-selected observations had substantially lower leverage than the injected good-leverage observations but much larger residual magnitudes and absolute DFBETA values (Figure~\ref{fig:02A-good-leverage-mechanism} and Table~\ref{tab:02-good-leverage-mechanism}). For example, under normal errors the median leverage ratio was (0.07), whereas the median absolute DFBETA and residual ratios were (19.19) and (131.91), respectively. MIS therefore replaced observations that were extreme in predictor space but approximately aligned with the fitted relation with observations that exerted greater influence on the target coefficient.

	\centering
	\includegraphics[width=0.92\textwidth]{../../output/02_distributional/figures/main/02_fig3_good_leverage_mechanism.pdf}
	\caption{Good-leverage mechanism diagnostics. Ratios compare the MIS-selected set with the injected good-leverage set; zero on the logarithmic ratio scale denotes equality.}
	\label{fig:02A-good-leverage-mechanism}
\end{figure}

\input{../../output/02_distributional/tables/main/02_tab3_good_leverage_mechanism.tex}

The iteration-level leverage--residual relationship provides the same interpretation across the complete distributional designs (Figure~\ref{fig:02A-leverage-residual-scatter}). Most observations lie to the left of the leverage-equality line and above the residual-equality line, showing that MIS generally selected sets with lower leverage but substantially larger residual magnitudes than the injected good-leverage sets. Recovery increased when the injected observations themselves became more similar to the coefficient-influential sets identified by MIS, most visibly under GPD errors. This pattern confirms that MIS responds to joint coefficient influence rather than leverage alone.

\subsubsection{EVT Calibration, Convergence, and Computational Diagnostics}
\label{app:detection-evt-diagnostics}

The EVT component remained well calibrated under no contamination. Its empirical rejection rate was (4.5\%), close to the nominal (5\%) level (Table~\ref{tab:02-empirical-size-summary}). Under the same set-level rejection definition, the corresponding classical threshold events occurred considerably more frequently. This distinction supports the use of the EVT reference distribution when deciding whether the coefficient influence of an entire candidate set is unusually large.

\input{../../output/02_distributional/tables/main/02_tab2_empirical_size_summary.tex}

Convergence was uniformly high across the design grid and therefore does not explain the differences in detection performance. Rates remained between (99.1\%) and (100.0\%) across sample sizes, contamination proportions, and contamination mechanisms (Figure~\ref{fig:02A-evt-convergence} and Table~\ref{tab:02A-evt-convergence-grid}). The modest reduction at the largest sample size and contamination proportion was common across contaminated and uncontaminated designs and remained quantitatively small.

\begin{figure}[H]
	\centering
	\includegraphics[width=0.95\textwidth]{../../output/02_distributional/figures/supplement/02_figA2_evt_convergence_heatmap.pdf}
	\caption{EVT convergence rates across sample sizes, contamination proportions, and contamination mechanisms.}
	\label{fig:02A-evt-convergence}
\end{figure}

\input{../../output/02_distributional/tables/supplement/02_tabA2_evt_convergence_grid.tex}

The distribution-level EVT summaries further distinguish coefficient-relevant from coefficient-benign contamination (Table~\ref{tab:02A-gev-summary}). Under no contamination, rejection rates ranged from (4.0\%) to (5.3\%) across the error distributions. Under bad leverage, they exceeded (94\%) for every distribution except GPD, for which the rejection rate was (58.0\%). In contrast, good-leverage rejection remained between (1.0\%) and (3.4\%), consistent with its alignment with the fitted regression relation. The much larger GPD scale and location estimates reflect the scale of that heavy-tailed design and should not be compared directly with the corresponding parameters from the other error distributions.

\input{../../output/02_distributional/tables/supplement/02_tabA4_gev_summary.tex}

Finally, adaptive block selection preserved computational feasibility as the influential-set size increased (Table~\ref{tab:02A-block-count-summary}). The full (50) blocks were retained at contamination proportions of (0.5\%) and (1\%), while the median count declined to approximately (39)--(40) at (2.5\%), (19) at (5\%), and (9) at (10\%). Although the highest-contamination configurations therefore used fewer than ten blocks, their convergence rates remained above (99\%), indicating that the reduced block count did not produce substantial numerical instability.

\subsection{Supplementary Estimation Results}
\label{app:estimation-results}

\subsubsection{Complete Estimator Comparisons}
\label{app:estimation-comparisons}

The complete estimator comparisons reinforce the concentration and tail patterns reported in Section~\ref{subsec:estimation-performance}. LTS produced the smallest mean absolute bias under every contamination mechanism, ranging from \(0.004\) to \(0.009\), with RMSE between \(0.008\) and \(0.020\). The MM-estimator was the next most stable procedure, with mean absolute bias between \(0.009\) and \(0.020\), although its RMSE ranged from \(0.137\) to \(0.342\). By contrast, the arithmetic means for OLS, the diagnostic-deletion estimators, and the MIS-based estimators were strongly affected by a small number of extremely large coefficient estimates (Tables~\ref{tab:robust-mean-coefficients}--\ref{tab:robust-rmse}). Figure~\ref{fig:04A-absolute-bias} confirms that these large pooled values primarily reflect heavy upper tails rather than uniformly poor estimation across all Monte Carlo draws.

The coverage results show that accurate point estimation and nominal interval coverage did not always coincide. Under bad leverage, OLS coverage fell to \(12.2\%\), while MIS-SAP achieved \(64.8\%\) and the MM-estimator and LTS achieved \(94.7\%\) and \(92.9\%\), respectively (Table~\ref{tab:robust-coverage}). Under good leverage, however, coverage declined to \(72.5\%\) for the MM-estimator and \(69.3\%\) for LTS despite their comparatively small point-estimation errors. Coverage must therefore be interpreted together with bias and RMSE rather than as an independent ranking of estimator quality.

\subsubsection{Variation across Data-Generating Processes}
\label{app:estimation-dgp}

The error distribution was the principal source of the extreme pooled estimation errors. Mean absolute bias remained comparatively small for most estimators under the normal, mixed-normal, beta-logistic, skewed-\(t\), contaminated, GOLM, and Pareto errors, but increased by several orders of magnitude under GPD errors (Figure~\ref{fig:04A-bias-error}). The extreme GPD cells consequently dominate the arithmetic summaries reported in Tables~\ref{tab:robust-mean-absolute-bias} and~\ref{tab:robust-rmse}.

\begin{figure}[H]

	\caption{Mean absolute coefficient bias by estimator, error distribution, and contamination mechanism.}
	\label{fig:04A-bias-error}
\end{figure}

Predictor contamination became especially consequential when it was combined with bad leverage. Figure~\ref{fig:04A-bias-x} shows that the contaminated-predictor designs generated the largest non-GPD biases under bad leverage, whereas predictor-distribution differences were more limited under no contamination, vertical outliers, and good leverage. Thus, the deterioration depended on the interaction between the predictor distribution and the contamination mechanism rather than on predictor contamination alone.

\subsubsection{Selected Set Size and Detection Overlap}
\label{app:estimation-selection}

The selection procedures differed substantially in the number of observations removed. The classical diagnostics commonly selected large fractions of the sample: the mean selected set ranged from approximately \(113\) to \(149\) observations for Cook's distance, from \(245\) to \(331\) for leverage, and from \(66\) to \(163\) for DFBETAS. In contrast, the data-adaptive MIS procedures generally selected relatively small sets outside bad-leverage contamination (Figure~\ref{fig:04A-selected-k} and Table~\ref{tab:robust-selected-k}). Under bad leverage, the mean selected set increased to \(33.82\) for adaptive-\(k\) MIS, \(40.36\) for iterative peeling, and \(58.53\) for MIS-SAP, indicating that these procedures responded more strongly when the contamination altered the target coefficient.

\begin{figure}[H]
	\centering
	\includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{../../output/04_robust/figures/main/04_fig3_selected_k_distributions.pdf}
	\caption{Distributions of the number of observations selected or removed by each detection procedure across contamination mechanisms.}
	\label{fig:04A-selected-k}
\end{figure}

Detection overlap was similarly mechanism dependent. Under bad leverage, the injected-set recovery rates were \(65.7\%\) for adaptive-\(k\) MIS, \(75.0\%\) for iterative peeling, \(58.2\%\) for MIS-SAP, and \(87.8\%\) for oracle-\(k\) MIS (Figure~\ref{fig:04A-overlap} and Table~\ref{tab:robust-detection-overlap}). MIS overlap was much lower under vertical outliers and good leverage, consistent with its focus on observations that jointly alter the target coefficient rather than on generic residual or leverage extremeness. These selection results explain how the estimators formed their retained samples, but they do not by themselves determine the accuracy of the resulting coefficient estimates.

\subsubsection{MIS-SAP Selection and Stopping Diagnostics}
\label{app:mis-sap-diagnostics}

The MIS-SAP stopping behaviour differed sharply between bad leverage and the other contamination mechanisms. Its reported detection rate was \(87.9\%\) under bad leverage, compared with \(4.6\%\) under no contamination, \(4.5\%\) under vertical outliers, and \(3.0\%\) under good leverage (Table~\ref{tab:robust-sap-diagnostics}). These proportions coincide with the shares of runs terminating at the maximum iteration limit in Table~\ref{tab:robust-sap-stop-reasons}. Thus, under bad leverage the global test frequently remained active through the iteration limit, whereas most runs in the other scenarios stopped because the set-level statistic was not significant.

\input{../../output/04_robust/tables/main/04_tab4_sap_diagnostics.tex}

The \(p\)-value distributions support this interpretation. The mean final global \(p\)-value was \(0.076\) under bad leverage, compared with \(0.501\) under no contamination, \(0.515\) under vertical outliers, and \(0.698\) under good leverage. Figure~\ref{fig:04A-sap-pvalues} additionally shows the distinction between the minimum and final global \(p\)-values, indicating that some runs contained an influential intermediate candidate set even when the final stopping decision was not significant.

\begin{figure}[H]
	\centering
	\includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{../../output/04_robust/figures/supplement/04_figA5_sap_pvalue_distributions.pdf}
	\caption{Distributions of the minimum and final MIS-SAP global \(p\)-values by contamination mechanism.}
	\label{fig:04A-sap-pvalues}
\end{figure}

The stopping-reason distribution makes the contrast explicit. Under bad leverage, \(87.9\%\) of runs reached \texttt{max\_iter}, whereas between \(95.4\%\) and \(97.0\%\) of runs under no contamination, vertical outliers, and good leverage stopped with \texttt{not\_significant} (Figure~\ref{fig:04A-sap-stops} and Table~\ref{tab:robust-sap-stop-reasons}). The large maximum-iteration share under bad leverage should therefore be interpreted as persistent evidence of coefficient influence rather than as a recorded computational error.

\begin{figure}[H]
	\centering
	\includegraphics[width=0.85\textwidth]{../../output/04_robust/figures/supplement/04_figA7_sap_stop_reasons.pdf}
	\caption{Distribution of MIS-SAP stopping reasons by contamination mechanism.}
	\label{fig:04A-sap-stops}
\end{figure}

\input{../../output/04_robust/tables/supplement/04_tabA4_sap_stop_reasons.tex}

The distribution-specific results identify the conditions behind the aggregate MIS-SAP findings. Under bad leverage and non-GPD errors, detection was between \(99.3\%\) and \(100.0\%\), the mean selected set was approximately \(66\)--\(69\), and mean absolute bias ranged from \(0.183\) to \(0.341\). Nevertheless, coverage remained between \(58.3\%\) and \(62.3\%\), showing that frequent detection did not fully restore inferential reliability. Under bad leverage with GPD errors, detection fell to \(4.0\%\), the mean selected set fell to \(0.05\), and mean absolute bias increased to approximately \(5.63\times10^{10}\). GPD errors also produced extremely large bias under vertical-outlier and good-leverage designs, despite an error rate of \(0.0\%\) throughout (Table~\ref{tab:robust-sap-error-distribution}). These results distinguish statistical deterioration from failure to return a finite computational result.

\input{../../output/04_robust/tables/supplement/04_tabA3_sap_by_error_distribution.tex}

MIS-SAP often reduced mean absolute bias relative to OLS in the most difficult cells, particularly under bad leverage, but the size of this relative improvement must be interpreted alongside the absolute error level. Positive values in Figure~\ref{fig:04A-sap-bias-advantage} indicate lower mean absolute bias for MIS-SAP than for OLS. Some of the largest positive differences occur in GPD cells because both procedures produced extremely large errors and MIS-SAP was less inaccurate in relative terms. An advantage over OLS in these cells therefore does not imply that MIS-SAP achieved reliable absolute estimation.

\subsubsection{Computational Performance and Numerical Availability}
\label{app:estimation-computation}

The numerical-availability checks show that the extreme coefficient errors were not caused by missing or non-finite outputs from the MIS procedures. All coefficient, coverage, and runtime values were finite for OLS, the diagnostic-deletion estimators, the four MIS procedures, and LTS. The MM-estimator returned finite coefficient and coverage values in \(99.6\%\) of the \(9{,}600\) recorded rows (Table~\ref{tab:robust-method-health}). Thus, the large errors in the demanding DGPs reflect the statistical behaviour of finite estimates rather than widespread numerical non-convergence.

\input{../../output/04_robust/tables/supplement/04_tabA2_method_health.tex}

The runtime comparisons reveal a substantial computational cost for iterative peeling under bad leverage. Its mean runtime increased from \(5.562\) seconds without contamination to \(56.919\) seconds under bad leverage. In contrast, adaptive-\(k\) MIS and oracle-\(k\) MIS required approximately \(1.37\)--\(1.53\) seconds, while MIS-SAP required approximately \(1.85\)--\(1.95\) seconds across the four mechanisms (Figure~\ref{fig:04A-runtime} and Table~\ref{tab:robust-runtime}). LTS was considerably faster at approximately \(0.11\) seconds per draw, although all MIS procedures were substantially more computationally demanding than OLS and the classical diagnostics.

At the nominal \(5\%\) level, empirical rejection was below the nominal benchmark for both GEV fitting methods, and the rejection curves remained below the diagonal across the considered significance levels (Figure~\ref{fig:03-null-calibration} and Table~\ref{tab:03-null-calibration}). \subsubsection{Nestedness, Collinearity, and Block-Count Diagnostics}
\label{app:nestedness-collinearity-blocks}

The selected influential sets were mostly stable as the target set size increased, but exact refinement introduced occasional non-nested transitions in more complex designs. The nestedness traces show high Jaccard similarity between consecutive selected sets in the simple linear and interaction designs, with larger instability in the collinear interaction and partially linear nonlinear designs (Figure~\ref{fig:03A-nestedness-traces} and Table~\ref{tab:03A-nestedness-summary}). This suggests that the selected set path is generally smooth, although some designs require caution when interpreting adjacent values of \(k\).
