\section{Simulation Results}
\label{sec:simulation}

This section evaluates the MIS framework through two complementary simulation exercises. The first exercise studies outlier detection: it asks whether MIS and classical diagnostics recover the observations that were deliberately injected into the sample. The second exercise studies coefficient estimation after diagnostic removal: it asks whether deleting the observations selected by each method and refitting the model improves recovery of the true slope coefficient. These two exercises are related but not identical, because a method can successfully flag unusual observations without necessarily producing the most accurate post-deletion estimator. Throughout the section, the tables and figures retain the shorter simulation labels ``vertical outliers'', ``good leverage'', and ``bad leverage''. In the text, these are interpreted as response outliers, on-model high-leverage observations, and slope-distorting high-leverage observations, respectively. The revised terminology is used to emphasize the substantive role of each scenario rather than to change the underlying simulation design.

The simulation design uses controlled contamination scenarios to separate different meanings of an outlier. Response outliers shift the outcome while leaving predictor values unchanged, representing observations with unusually large residuals but no unusual covariate position. On-model high-leverage observations move far in predictor space but remain approximately aligned with the fitted regression relation, representing unusual but internally consistent observations. Slope-distorting high-leverage observations move far in predictor space and also shift the outcome in a direction that can rotate the fitted slope. This distinction matters because MIS is designed to identify the subset with the largest effect on the target coefficient, whereas classical diagnostics respond more directly to residual size, leverage, or single-observation coefficient changes. The detection results should therefore be interpreted as a comparison between coefficient-targeted outlier detection and more general forms of residual or leverage screening.

\subsection{Outlier Detection}
\label{subsec:detection-performance}

MIS can be used as an outlier detection tool when an outlier is defined by its effect on the target coefficient rather than only by its residual size or leverage. Classical diagnostics answer related but different questions: Cook's distance responds to single-observation changes in the fitted model, leverage identifies unusual predictor values, and DFBETAS measures single-observation coefficient sensitivity. MIS instead asks which set of observations would jointly move the coefficient of interest the most if removed. The comparison below therefore evaluates whether coefficient-targeted detection recovers the injected observations that actually threaten the slope, while also showing when classical diagnostics are better suited to detecting other forms of unusualness.


The detection results show that MIS was strongest when the injected observations jointly distorted the coefficient of interest. Under slope-distorting high-leverage contamination, labelled as bad leverage in the tables and figures, MIS recovered an average of \(92.9\%\) of the injected observations, compared with \(76.0\%\) for the strongest classical diagnostic, corresponding to an advantage of \(16.9\) percentage points (Table~\ref{tab:02-detection-power-summary}). This advantage remained positive throughout the grid of sample sizes and contamination proportions, ranging from approximately \(8.0\) to \(14.9\) percentage points in Figure~\ref{fig:02-mis-advantage}. Thus, MIS provided its clearest detection gain in the setting where influence on the target coefficient is most relevant.

This advantage did not extend to all forms of contamination. MIS recovered \(26.6\%\) of response outliers and \(36.9\%\) of on-model high-leverage observations, which were respectively \(21.2\) and \(33.2\) percentage points below the best classical diagnostic. The heatmap shows that this weaker recovery was systematic: the MIS advantage ranged from \(-36.0\) to \(-12.8\) percentage points for vertical outliers and from \(-45.2\) to \(-21.9\) percentage points for good leverage. These lower recovery rates reflect the objective of MIS. Large response shocks and leverage aligned with the empirical regression relation can be outlying in a residual or covariate sense without materially changing the target coefficient. Therefore, MIS should not be interpreted as a general-purpose outlier detector. Its strength is targeted detection of observations that jointly alter the coefficient of interest, and this strength is most visible under slope-distorting high-leverage contamination.

The near-uniform EVT convergence rate of \(99.7\%\) across contaminated scenarios indicates that the observed selection pattern was not driven by numerical fitting failures. Instead, it is consistent with substantive differences between coefficient-targeted MIS selection and the broader notions of outlyingness measured by classical diagnostics. Complete distribution-specific comparisons and the diagnostic analysis of the good-leverage mechanism are reported in Appendix~\ref{app:simulation}.









\subsection{Coefficient Estimation after Diagnostic Removal}% 04
\label{subsec:estimation-performance}

The estimation exercise treats most detection methods as diagnostic-removal procedures rather than as standalone estimators. OLS is first fit to the contaminated sample. Cook's distance, leverage, DFBETAS, and the MIS-based procedures then select observations for removal, after which OLS is refit on the retained sample. The resulting coefficient is therefore a post-diagnostic OLS estimate. This construction differs from the MM-estimator and LTS, which are direct robust estimators that change the estimating criterion itself rather than deleting a selected set and refitting OLS. The MIS-based estimates should consequently be interpreted as hybrid deletion estimators: their performance depends not only on the MIS objective, but also on the rule used to choose the set size, the direction of coefficient movement, the stopping rule, and the final OLS refit.

The estimation comparison is therefore best read as a fixed stress test of post-diagnostic removal rules. In this exercise, the sample size is held at \(n=5000\), the injected set size is fixed at \(k=50\), and the contamination magnitude is fixed at \(10\). These settings create a demanding environment in which the selected deletion rule can materially affect the refitted slope. The comparison is not intended to exhaust all possible MIS-based estimators. In particular, alternative choices of \(k\), deletion direction, stopping rule, and refitting target could produce different finite-sample behaviour. The current MIS-SAP specification should also be interpreted as a single-step SAP-gated deletion rule, because the procedure allows one accepted SAP removal step before the final OLS refit. The results below therefore evaluate the specific deletion estimators implemented here rather than an intrinsic point-estimation property of MIS itself.

For point estimation, LTS was the most accurate and stable estimator within the considered simulation designs. Its mean absolute bias ranged from \(0.004\) to \(0.009\) across the four contamination mechanisms, with RMSE between \(0.008\) and \(0.020\). The MM-estimator was the next most stable procedure, with mean absolute bias between \(0.009\) and \(0.020\), although its RMSE was higher, ranging from \(0.137\) to \(0.342\) (Tables~\ref{tab:robust-mean-absolute-bias} and~\ref{tab:robust-rmse}). Figure~\ref{fig:04-coefficient-errors} shows the same pattern visually: the coefficient errors of LTS and the MM-estimator remained concentrated near zero under each contamination mechanism. Thus, direct robust estimation provided the most reliable point estimates in this stress-test comparison.

The diagnostic-removal estimators were less stable because their performance depended on the selected deletion set. The MIS-based estimators, OLS, and the estimators based on classical diagnostic deletion exhibited substantially longer error tails in Figure~\ref{fig:04-coefficient-errors}. For several MIS procedures, the medians remained close to zero while the arithmetic means were displaced by a small number of extremely large estimates. This pattern indicates that their pooled errors were driven primarily by rare tail failures rather than by persistent central bias. The main distinction is therefore between central performance, where many deletion estimators often remained close to the true coefficient, and tail reliability, where direct robust estimators were more stable.

Taken together, these results show that direct robust estimators were the most reliable point-estimation procedures in the stress-test design, whereas post-diagnostic deletion estimators were more vulnerable to rare but severe tail failures. The next subsection examines where those tail failures occurred and whether they reflected statistical instability or numerical non-completion.

\subsection{Failure Modes and Numerical Availability}
\label{subsec:failure-modes}

The large errors of MIS-SAP were concentrated in a small number of identifiable data-generating conditions rather than being uniform across the simulation grid. Outside the GPD designs and the combination of contaminated predictors with slope-distorting high-leverage contamination, labelled as bad leverage in the tables and figures, the probability that \(\lvert\widehat{\beta}-\beta_0\rvert>0.5\) was generally zero and did not exceed \(10\%\). In contrast, this probability reached \(100\%\) under GPD errors for every predictor distribution and contamination mechanism. Under slope-distorting high-leverage contamination with contaminated predictors, it ranged from \(50\%\) to \(59\%\) across the non-GPD error distributions (Figure~\ref{fig:04-mis-sap-large-errors}). Figure~\ref{fig:04-mis-sap-large-errors} therefore explains the long tails in Figure~\ref{fig:04-coefficient-errors}: the poor aggregate performance of MIS-SAP was driven by extreme-tail errors and contaminated-predictor leverage designs, not by uniform deterioration across all scenarios.

These failure modes were statistical rather than computational. Although the recorded MIS-SAP error rate was \(0.0\%\) across all contamination mechanisms, this result only confirms successful completion of the procedure and leaves coefficient accuracy to be assessed by the bias and coverage results. Under slope-distorting high-leverage contamination and non-GPD errors, the mean absolute bias ranged from \(0.183\) to \(0.341\), while empirical coverage fell to between \(58.3\%\) and \(62.3\%\). Under GPD errors, the mean absolute bias became extremely large across all contamination mechanisms. The deterioration therefore reflects sensitivity to extreme tails and structurally influential contamination, with the numerical implementation remaining available. Complete MIS-SAP selection, stopping, and computational diagnostics are reported in Appendix~\ref{app:simulation}.
