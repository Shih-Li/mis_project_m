\section{Simulation Results}
\label{sec:simulation}

\subsection{Detection Performance}
\label{subsec:detection-performance}

The detection pattern was consistent with the coefficient-specific objective of MIS rather than with generic outlier identification. Under bad leverage, MIS recovered an average of (92.9\%) of the injected observations, compared with (76.0\%) for the best-performing classical diagnostic, corresponding to an advantage of (16.9) percentage points (Table~\ref{tab:02-detection-power-summary}). This advantage remained positive throughout the sample-size and contamination grid, ranging from approximately (8.0) to (14.9) percentage points in Figure~\ref{fig:02-mis-advantage}. In contrast, MIS recovered (26.6\%) of vertical outliers and (36.9\%) of good-leverage observations, respectively (21.2) and (33.2) percentage points below the best classical diagnostic. These lower recovery rates are expected because vertical displacement and leverage aligned with the empirical regression relation need not materially influence the coefficient of interest. The heatmap shows that this selectivity was systematic: the MIS advantage ranged from (-36.0) to (-12.8) percentage points for vertical outliers and from (-45.2) to (-21.9) percentage points for good leverage. Thus, the higher overlap achieved by classical diagnostics in these settings reflects their sensitivity to generic residual or leverage extremeness, whereas MIS concentrates on observations that materially alter the target coefficient. Taken together, the results show that MIS responds most strongly to bad-leverage contamination, for which the injected observations jointly distort the coefficient of interest.

The near-uniform EVT convergence rate of (99.7\%) across contaminated scenarios indicates that the observed selection pattern was not driven by numerical fitting failures. Instead, it is consistent with substantive differences between coefficient-targeted MIS selection and the broader notions of outlyingness measured by classical diagnostics. Complete distribution-specific comparisons and the diagnostic analysis of the good-leverage mechanism are reported in Appendix~\ref{app}.









\subsection{Estimation Performance}% 04
\label{subsec:estimation-performance}

For point estimation, LTS was the most accurate and stable estimator within the considered simulation designs. Its mean absolute bias ranged from \(0.004\) to \(0.009\) across the four contamination mechanisms, with RMSE between \(0.008\) and \(0.020\). The MM-estimator was the next most stable procedure, with mean absolute bias between \(0.009\) and \(0.020\), although its RMSE was higher, ranging from \(0.137\) to \(0.342\) (Tables~\ref{tab:robust-mean-absolute-bias} and~\ref{tab:robust-rmse}). Figure~\ref{fig:04-coefficient-errors} shows that the coefficient errors of LTS and the MM-estimator remained concentrated near zero under each contamination mechanism. In contrast, OLS, the diagnostic-deletion estimators, and the MIS-based estimators exhibited substantially longer tails. For several MIS procedures, the medians remained close to zero while the arithmetic means were displaced by a small number of extremely large estimates, indicating that their pooled errors were driven primarily by tail failures rather than by persistent central bias.

The large errors of MIS-SAP were concentrated in a small number of identifiable data-generating conditions rather than being uniform across the simulation grid. Outside the GPD designs and the combination of contaminated predictors with bad-leverage contamination, the probability that \(\lvert\widehat{\beta}-\beta_0\rvert>0.5\) was generally zero and did not exceed \(10\%\). In contrast, this probability reached \(100\%\) under GPD errors for every predictor distribution and contamination mechanism. Under bad leverage with contaminated predictors, it ranged from \(50\%\) to \(59\%\) across the non-GPD error distributions (Figure~\ref{fig:04-mis-sap-large-errors}). Consequently, the large pooled bias and RMSE values for MIS-SAP are dominated by these extreme-tail and contaminated-predictor configurations and should not be interpreted as representative of its performance in most design cells.

The main failures occurred under GPD errors and under bad-leverage contamination combined with contaminated predictors. These settings represent applications with extremely heavy-tailed outcomes, such as rare but very large financial losses, insurance claims, or demand shocks, and data containing a mixture of subpopulations or mismeasured covariates together with observations that lie far from the prevailing regression relation. MIS-SAP is therefore least reliable when extreme outcome tails and structurally influential predictor contamination occur simultaneously.

Although the recorded MIS-SAP error rate was \(0.0\%\) across all contamination mechanisms, this result indicates only that the procedure completed without a recorded computational error; it does not imply accurate coefficient estimation. Estimation reliability nevertheless deteriorated in the most demanding designs. Under bad leverage and non-GPD errors, the mean absolute bias ranged from \(0.183\) to \(0.341\), while empirical coverage fell to between \(58.3\%\) and \(62.3\%\). Under GPD errors, the mean absolute bias became extremely large across all contamination mechanisms. The deterioration therefore reflects statistical sensitivity to extreme tails and structurally influential contamination rather than a failure of the numerical implementation. Complete MIS-SAP selection, stopping, and computational diagnostics are reported in Appendix~\ref{app:simulation}.
