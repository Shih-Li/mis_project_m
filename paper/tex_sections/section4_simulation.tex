\section{Simulation Results}
\label{sec:simulation}

\subsection{Detection Performance}
\label{subsec:detection-performance}

The detection results show that MIS was strongest when the injected observations jointly distorted the coefficient of interest. Under bad leverage, MIS recovered an average of \(92.9\%\) of the injected observations, compared with \(76.0\%\) for the best-performing classical diagnostic, corresponding to an advantage of \(16.9\) percentage points (Table~\ref{tab:02-detection-power-summary}). This advantage remained positive throughout the sample-size and contamination grid, ranging from approximately \(8.0\) to \(14.9\) percentage points in Figure~\ref{fig:02-mis-advantage}. Thus, MIS provided its clearest detection gain in the setting where coefficient-targeted influence is most relevant.

This advantage did not extend to all forms of contamination. MIS recovered \(26.6\%\) of vertical outliers and \(36.9\%\) of good-leverage observations, which were respectively \(21.2\) and \(33.2\) percentage points below the best classical diagnostic. The heatmap shows that this weaker recovery was systematic: the MIS advantage ranged from \(-36.0\) to \(-12.8\) percentage points for vertical outliers and from \(-45.2\) to \(-21.9\) percentage points for good leverage. These lower recovery rates reflect the objective of MIS. Vertical displacement and leverage aligned with the empirical regression relation can be outlying without materially changing the target coefficient. Therefore, MIS should not be interpreted as a general-purpose outlier detector. Its strength is targeted detection of observations that jointly alter the coefficient of interest, and this strength is most visible under bad-leverage contamination.

The near-uniform EVT convergence rate of \(99.7\%\) across contaminated scenarios indicates that the observed selection pattern was not driven by numerical fitting failures. Instead, it is consistent with substantive differences between coefficient-targeted MIS selection and the broader notions of outlyingness measured by classical diagnostics. Complete distribution-specific comparisons and the diagnostic analysis of the good-leverage mechanism are reported in Appendix~\ref{app:simulation}.









\subsection{Estimation Performance}% 04
\label{subsec:estimation-performance}

For point estimation, LTS was the most accurate and stable estimator within the considered simulation designs. Its mean absolute bias ranged from \(0.004\) to \(0.009\) across the four contamination mechanisms, with RMSE between \(0.008\) and \(0.020\). The MM-estimator was the next most stable procedure, with mean absolute bias between \(0.009\) and \(0.020\), although its RMSE was higher, ranging from \(0.137\) to \(0.342\) (Tables~\ref{tab:robust-mean-absolute-bias} and~\ref{tab:robust-rmse}). Figure~\ref{fig:04-coefficient-errors} shows the same pattern visually: the coefficient errors of LTS and the MM-estimator remained concentrated near zero under each contamination mechanism.

The MIS-based estimators did not match the point-estimation stability of LTS or the MM-estimator. Together with OLS and the diagnostic-deletion estimators, they exhibited substantially longer error tails in Figure~\ref{fig:04-coefficient-errors}. For several MIS procedures, the medians remained close to zero while the arithmetic means were displaced by a small number of extremely large estimates. This pattern indicates that their pooled errors were driven primarily by tail failures rather than by persistent central bias.

The large errors of MIS-SAP were concentrated in a small number of identifiable data-generating conditions rather than being uniform across the simulation grid. Outside the GPD designs and the combination of contaminated predictors with bad-leverage contamination, the probability that \(\lvert\widehat{\beta}-\beta_0\rvert>0.5\) was generally zero and did not exceed \(10\%\). In contrast, this probability reached \(100\%\) under GPD errors for every predictor distribution and contamination mechanism. Under bad leverage with contaminated predictors, it ranged from \(50\%\) to \(59\%\) across the non-GPD error distributions (Figure~\ref{fig:04-mis-sap-large-errors}). These results show that MIS-SAP was reliable in many regular cells but fragile under extreme-tail errors and contaminated-predictor bad leverage.

The failure cases have a clear practical interpretation. GPD errors represent applications with extremely heavy-tailed outcomes, such as rare but very large financial losses, insurance claims, or demand shocks. Contaminated predictors under bad leverage represent settings with a mixture of subpopulations or mismeasured covariates together with observations that lie far from the prevailing regression relation. Although the recorded MIS-SAP error rate was \(0.0\%\) across all contamination mechanisms, this result indicates only that the procedure completed without a recorded computational error; it does not imply accurate coefficient estimation. Under bad leverage and non-GPD errors, the mean absolute bias ranged from \(0.183\) to \(0.341\), while empirical coverage fell to between \(58.3\%\) and \(62.3\%\). Under GPD errors, the mean absolute bias became extremely large across all contamination mechanisms. The deterioration therefore reflects statistical sensitivity to extreme tails and structurally influential contamination rather than a failure of the numerical implementation. Complete MIS-SAP selection, stopping, and computational diagnostics are reported in Appendix~\ref{app:simulation}.
